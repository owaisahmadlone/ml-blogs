Retrieval-augmented generation is deceptively easy to prototype and genuinely
hard to ship. You can wire up an embedding model, a vector database, and an LLM
in an afternoon and get something that demos beautifully. Then real users show
up with real questions, and the cracks appear.

This is the guide I wish I'd had when I started building RAG systems at
[Landeed](https://www.landeed.com). It's opinionated, and it focuses on the
parts that actually move the needle.

## The mental model

RAG is not one system — it's two, stitched together:

1. A **retrieval** system whose only job is to find the right context.
2. A **generation** system whose only job is to answer given that context.

Most teams obsess over the generation half (prompt engineering, model choice)
when their real problem lives in retrieval. A perfect model cannot answer from
context it never received.

> If your answers are wrong, first ask: *was the right information even in the
> context window?* You'll be surprised how often the answer is no.

## Chunking is a modeling decision

The naive approach — split every document into 512-token windows — throws away
structure that your retriever desperately needs.

- **Respect document boundaries.** Sections, headings, and list items carry
  semantic meaning. Splitting mid-sentence or mid-table destroys it.
- **Overlap, but not too much.** A little overlap (10–15%) preserves context at
  boundaries. Too much inflates your index and returns near-duplicate chunks.
- **Attach metadata.** Title, section path, source, and date should travel with
  every chunk. Half of production retrieval bugs are solved by filtering on
  metadata, not by better embeddings.

<Callout type="tip">
  Store the *heading path* of each chunk (e.g. "Guide › Chunking › Overlap") and
  prepend it to the chunk text before embedding. Retrieval quality jumps because
  the embedding now knows where the text lives in the document.
</Callout>

## Retrieval: dense is not enough

Pure vector search is great at semantic similarity and terrible at exact
matches — product codes, names, error strings, anything where the *token itself*
matters. The fix is hybrid retrieval.

```python
def hybrid_search(query: str, k: int = 20) -> list[Chunk]:
    dense = vector_index.search(embed(query), k=k)
    sparse = bm25_index.search(query, k=k)
    # Reciprocal rank fusion combines the two rankings robustly
    return reciprocal_rank_fusion([dense, sparse], k=k)
```

Reciprocal rank fusion is the boring, reliable default. It needs no tuning of
score scales and consistently beats either retriever alone.

### Reranking earns its keep

Retrieve broadly (say, top 20), then use a cross-encoder reranker to select the
best 3–5. A bi-encoder embeds query and document *separately*; a cross-encoder
sees them *together* and is far more accurate at judging relevance.

The cost is latency, so rerank a shortlist, never the whole corpus.

## Evals or it didn't happen

You cannot improve what you don't measure, and "it feels better" is not a
metric. Build an eval set *before* you start tuning.

- **Retrieval metrics:** recall@k and MRR against a set of query → known-relevant
  chunk pairs. This isolates retrieval quality from generation quality.
- **End-to-end metrics:** faithfulness (is the answer grounded in the context?)
  and answer relevance. LLM-as-judge works well here if you calibrate it against
  a few dozen human labels.

<Callout type="warning">
  An eval set of 30 real questions beats an eval set of 3,000 synthetic ones.
  Pull yours from actual user logs the moment you have them.
</Callout>

## Failure modes you will hit

- **The confidently wrong answer.** The model answers from parametric memory when
  retrieval returns nothing useful. Fix: instruct it to say "I don't know" and
  test that it actually does.
- **Lost in the middle.** Models attend less to the middle of long contexts. Put
  the most relevant chunk first, and don't stuff 40 chunks in hoping for the
  best.
- **Stale index.** Your data changed; your embeddings didn't. Version your index
  and plan for re-embedding when you change chunking or the embedding model.

## What I'd tell my past self

Start with the simplest thing that could work — hybrid retrieval, a reranker, a
tight prompt — and *instrument everything*. Most of the wins come from looking at
failed queries one by one, not from swapping in a fancier model.

RAG rewards patience and good taste far more than clever architecture. Build the
eval loop first, and the rest follows.
