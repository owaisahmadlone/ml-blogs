When people picture an AI engineer at a startup, they imagine someone training
models all day. The reality is more interesting and more varied. At an early
company, the model is frequently the *easiest* part of the problem.

Here's what the job actually involves, and what I've learned doing it.

## You own the whole pipeline

There is no ML platform team to hand things off to. You are the data pipeline,
the training code, the evals, the serving infrastructure, and the on-call
engineer when inference latency spikes at 2am.

This is not a complaint — it's the appeal. Owning the whole pipeline means you
see where value actually leaks out of the system, which is almost never where you
expected.

## Data beats models, again and again

The highest-leverage thing I do is rarely "pick a better model." It's:

- Finding the mislabeled examples poisoning an eval set.
- Noticing that 20% of production traffic is a use case nobody designed for.
- Building the tooling so a domain expert can label 200 examples in an hour
  instead of a day.

<Callout type="tip">
  Spend a day building the internal tool that lets a non-engineer inspect and
  correct model outputs. It will pay for itself within a week and keep paying.
</Callout>

## Ship, measure, repeat

Startups win on iteration speed, not model sophistication. The instinct to
perfect a model before shipping is usually wrong. A worse model in production,
generating real feedback, beats a better model in a notebook.

That means:

- **Instrument first.** If you can't measure it in production, you can't improve
  it. Logging and evals come before optimization.
- **Prefer boring technology.** A managed vector DB and a well-known model let you
  spend your innovation budget where it differentiates the product.
- **Make rollback trivial.** You will ship regressions. The teams that move fast
  are the ones that can undo mistakes in minutes.

## The skills that compound

Technical depth matters, but the things that have compounded most for me are
oddly mundane:

1. **Writing clearly.** Half of engineering is convincing other people —
   proposals, postmortems, docs. Clear writing is clear thinking made visible.
2. **Scoping ruthlessly.** Knowing what *not* to build is worth more than any
   framework.
3. **Talking to users.** The best feature ideas came from watching someone
   struggle with the product, not from a research paper.

## Would I recommend it?

If you like ambiguity, breadth, and seeing your work reach users in days rather
than quarters — yes, without hesitation. If you want to go deep on one narrow
research problem for a year, a lab is a better fit.

The founding-engineer path trades depth for leverage. For me, that trade has been
more than worth it.
